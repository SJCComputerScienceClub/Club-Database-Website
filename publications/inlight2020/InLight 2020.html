Mission Statement

InLight is a student-led platform created to spark conversations, breathe nuance into the biased, and uplift voices that would otherwise go unheard. In the spirit of St. John Baptist de La Salle, InLight aims to serve our community by giving everyone a voice, no matter their background. We hope To unite our diverse DMV community through our common home: St. John's.

Letter from the Editor

Following InLight Magazines fall edition, I received comments from students, parents, teachers, and alumni on how beautiful the publication looked and how much they enjoyed reading the unique stories the editors had to tell. In our editors edition, the InLight editors told their stories in a raw and vulnerable light, discussing topics inspired by their backgrounds and passions. Readers found themselves relating to the universal messages of otherness, inclusion, celebration, and hope. All this positive feedback fueled my excitement to invite more members of the St. Johns community spread InLights mission. Continuing to inspire dialogue surrounding identity not only highlights the internal humanities that connect us, but also celebrates the wealth of individuality we are blessed to have in our Lasallian learning community. I hope as InLight begins to grow, so will ideals of diversity and inclusivity. As always, a huge thanks to Ms. Gelso, our teacher moderator, for her endless encouragement and support. As students, we all have so much potential to bring beneficial social change in the future, but who said we have to wait? Use your voice now. By embracing truths and dissembling false stigmas, empower yourself and those around you. Good luck and I hope you enjoy St. Johns InLight Winter 2020 edition!

Annie Phan 20, Editor in Chief

A Family Memory

Max Howard 20

Bang! . . . babaBang . . . BANG BANG! One thousand excited sparks fall down from the sky every ten seconds on the night of the Fourth of July each year... or so I have been told. I have grown up proud and rather patriotic for the freedom that America stands for, but I have witnessed this lush display of summer patriotism only once or twice in my life. Usually, my family is in Japan to visit my grandparents on the Fourth of July. Unfortunately, to my Japanese grandparents, July 4 and its BANG!s mean something much more sinister and terrifying. It is something that hurts to remember yet must be cemented into the minds of future generations. On July 4, 1945 at 2:56 a.m. in the dark, early morning, the city of Takamatsu was lit-up and filled with an exploding, life-stealing red color from incendiary bombs dropped by the infamous B-29 bombers. Both of my grandparents witnessed this attack; my grandfather had to escape from his house in the burning city-center, while my grandmother watched the destruction unfold in the distance.

74 years later, I sat and took in the havoc in the illustrations I was seeing: the planes carpet-bombing techniques, people dead in public tubs which they thought were refuge from fire, desperate pairs of eyes and dead expressions inside and around bomb shelters, and a sky filled with streaks of dreadful purple, grey, and black. These images were a part of an artists presentation at this years commemoration event, which focused on communicating the evils of war to future generations, something my grandfather strived to do as a vocal advocate for peace in the city before his unfortunate death this past May. My grandmother sat next to me firmly gripping a handkerchief, listening intently. Coupled with the news that U.S. relations with Iran were rapidly deteriorating and my previous experiences during a visit to the Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum, the commemorative event encouraged me to rethink how I approach conversations with my peers and broaden my view of the world around me.

As with the significance of July Fourth in my life, many situations have two sides of the story and understanding one side is never enough. For this reason, I have become more conscientious about cultivating an environment that promotes understanding, being open-minded, and listening attentively to other peoples stories and arguments. Among the politically diverse lunch tables at which I have sat, I have heard people jumping to conclusions and accusing other groups of not being compassionate, lacking common sense, or being too weak. I firmly believe that the less we are willing to listen and be open to arguments opposite our own, the more we alienate people and cause rifts in all levels of our life.

I treasure that I can think and speak freely, and I also treasure that I have the freedom to understand others. Knowing that a lack of international understanding could lead to another war, my grandfather wanted my generation to be actively against war by encouraging constructive dialogue about solutions to international affairs and other social problems. I believe I have a duty to continue his mission. I will be forever grateful for the fundamental freedoms celebrated on the Fourth of July, but I never want to forget my grandparents memories from July 4, 1945.

A Simple Letter

Bailey Middleton 21

The student sits in the chemistry classroom, pencil in hand. The student has studied all week for the final exam and knows the score she must get to maintain the good grade, the acceptable grade. The bell rings and it is time for the exam to begin. The teacher picks up the exams, ready to pass them out. First, the teacher begins to pray: let us remember we are in the holy presence of God. The class quiets down. The student bows her head, still focused on the exam which is to come. So focused to the point where she zones out, only catching the last part of the prayer: Lord let us recall all that we have learned this year while we are taking this exam, and let us remember that our grades do not define us. The teacher passes out the exam and the test begins. The student looks down at the paper and begins filling out the test. The student is confident and knows how to find the right answers. This is because the student is prepared, as a student should be, as a student must be to maintain her grade. The student finishes the exam.

A few minutes later the bell rings. Freedom! The girl waltzes out of the classroom. Its summer! Finally summer, finally time to forget about the grades which take over her life during the long months of school. She spends the summer working, she loves her job. She goes out with her friends. She reads what interests her, she sleeps when she wants, she learns without pressure, without having to remember every detail. She feels happy. She feels human. August comes around and the girl thinks maybe its time to start the summer projects. It is now late August and it is time for field hockey tryouts. She has worked hard all summer to make the team. On the last day of tryouts her coaches tell her she has made varsity. She is elated. She is ready to begin the season and to work hard at something she is good at, something that makes her happy.

It is now the day before school. The student rushes to finish the summer projects. Why didnt the student do them earlier like a good student, a prepared student? This is a bad way to start off the year. She has to have good grades. Has to get good letters, has to get into college, has to have good grades. Who is she if not for good grades? It is late now and the student goes to sleep. In the morning the student wakes. It is time to prepare for school. Her smile is gone. She longs to go back to the summer, the fun, the excitement. But it is time to go back to the place she hates. Back to working so hard for a simple letter which has come to define her.

Tamales

Erika Carmody 20

My name is Erika Kate Carmody, I have light brown hair and pale skin, and I am Mexican-American. Throughout my life, I have struggled with my identity and the realization of my place in the Latinx community. As a young child, being Mexican was not something that made me different or stand out. My name was Erika Kate Carmody, I was Mexican, I loved enchiladas, I wanted to learn about the dark side of the moon, and I was the most beautiful girl in the world. I was ready to take on the universe and was proud of every aspect of myself. However, when I started elementary school, my dreams and self identity began to be broken down and chipped away.

The food I brought to school was no longer just food, but something gross and foreign to my English and German classmates. I was no longer the most beautiful girl in the world, as my hairy arms and legs became compared to those of a man by my Aryan friends. In a desperate attempt to be accepted and erase my Latino roots, I began identifying as Irish and begged my mom to let me dye my hair blonde and shave my arms and legs. While I kept hopelessly running from my culture, it still managed to catch up with me every year.

Around Christmastime, my family as a whole makes three hundred tamales in the traditional Mexican fashion. The smell of the chile con carne cooking, the stickiness of the masa that got between my fingers when I messed up spreading it on the oja, and the disappointment you get when you accidentally bite into a sweet raisin tamale are ingrained in my mind. Instead of something I wanted to run away from, tamale making was something I looked forward to. Making tamales the same way as my mom, grandma, great-grandma, and everyone before her was akin to a rite of initiation to the Magaa side of the family.

Tamales are more than just a traditional dish in my family, they are the driving force of community. The tamales we dont eat or freeze the first night are either delivered to my neighbors, or eaten at a neighborhood dinner in my dining room. However, even though our community came together, I still felt left out. As some of my neighbors speak Spanish, I began to realize that I could barely communicate and could only say gracias when they complimented my cooking skills. I then decided that I wanted to improve my Spanish to truly be a part of the community.

Embarrassed by my lack of understanding, I began practicing my Spanish in private. From completing a Duolingo courses to watching TV shows in Spanish, I slowly grew more confident in my abilities. I began to respond in the same language when my grandma would talk to me in Spanish, turned off closed captions in the TV shows, and spoke Spanish at my job.

My newfound language skills truly made me feel a part of the Latinx community my senior year when I was presented with the opportunity to translate at parent-teacher conferences at the San Miguel School, where the majority of the parents are immigrants who only speak Spanish. Amongst the families at the San Miguel School, I saw glimpses of my own family. From the similar mannerisms of my mom and the mother and her son, to having conversations about life with the mother after the conference in Spanish, I finally felt like I truly belonged in the community in which I grew up.

Rosary

Artwork and Article by Ryon Sarkarzadeh 20

I was born in Ann Arbor, Michigan into a loving Middle Eastern family; however, I wasn't able to truly embrace my Iranian background until recently. Living in America, moving to DC, and going to school a different ethnicity than the majority of students came with its own set of challenges.

Everyday, kids would ask me where I was from and why I looked different from the standard blonde hair and blue eyes. Embarrassed, I would say I was from Michigan and the sun changed the color of my skin. This ultimately did not help to alleviate the pain I soon felt when I was faced with bullying later in my adolescence when my peers soon realized I had an Iranian background. They would attack me with a slew of racist insults and remarks and as a result, I began to hate my ethnicity. I completely disregarded my heritage while trying to assimilate to the culture that surrounded me. My indifference towards my culture turned it into a background dancer of my own music video.

I never truly appreciated my background until 7th grade when I was able to go to Iran and see my own ethnicity staring back at me. I went to visit my grandparents, but I came back with a newfound love for my culture. I was able to visit Persepolis and learn all about the Persian Empire. Its sheer magnitude and influence over the world showed me how strong these people, my people, were. I have my own community, my own people, and that is what makes me different amongst my peers. My ethnicity is the foundation for my dissimilitude and it is what made me who I am today.

Thats what I see when I look at the rosary. I was not born into a particular faith nor is it a significant part of my life, but a majority of my life has been surrounded by Christianity. The symbolism the rosary resembles what I want my life to embody. The amount of love and grace received through the rosary is how I want to feel when I think of my identity. The relationships it creates and the communities it builds show me that everyone has their own community, everyone has their own identity, they just need to find it. Finding love in yourself and your identity will allow to be a much more joyous human, and that is the only thing I think that matters in this world.

Spanish vs. Hispanic vs. Latinx

Amalia Villegas Vega 21

Spanish, Hispanic, and Latinx are all different words to describe ones identity and are often mixed up, even by the people who fit into one of these groups. It can be very confusing to understand a persons identity if you do not understand the words they use to identify themselves. I personally identify as Latina and Hispanic, but what does this even mean?

To start, Spanish means someone who comes from the country of Spain. In this case, a persons race and the language they speak does not really matter. Typically, if a person is Spanish, and their ancestors are from Spain, they are white. Spain is a country in Europe and people of European descent are white; therefore, people of Spanish descent are also white. The most important thing to remember is that Spanish has to do with someones nationality.

Hispanic has to do with the language you or your parents speak. Hispanic is defined as someone from a Spanish-speaking country. This includes Spain and a lot of Latin American countries that speak Spanish. Hispanic does not necessarily mean that the person speaks Spanish but they descend from a Spanish-speaking culture.

Latinx is defined as the gender-neutral term for someone from Latin America. This word is often interchanged with Hispanic, but there are a few nationalities that do not fall under both categories. For example, someone from Spain is Hispanic because they are from a Spanish-speaking country, but they are not Latinx because Spain is not part of Latin America. On the other hand, Brazilians are Latinx because the country is part of Latin America but not Spanish-speaking. Often times, Latinx is seen as a race, but there is truly a range because many Latinx are of mixed race ancestry. Most Latinx people can trace ancestors to Europe, mainly Portugal and Spain, Africa, and American indigenous peoples.

Spanish, Hispanic, and Latinx are three words that can define a persons identity but do not always cover race.

I am Latina and Hispanic and these two words are a big part of my identity. My parents are both from Medellin, Colombia, and being Colombian is a big part of my identity. I really appreciate my roots and love all aspects of my Colombian culture from the food to the obsession with soccer. I am proud of my strong connection to Colombia and love spending summers in Medellin visiting family. Being Latina and speaking Spanish is an amazing gift that allows me to have an instant connection with complete strangers with similar backgrounds.

Regaining Myself

Mia Mcllwain 23

Where has she gone? was one of my many thoughts in the beginning of freshman year. I was wondering where the person who used to be me was because the person I saw in the mirror was not me. In middle school, I was a very outgoing person who loved public speaking, makingn friends, and just socializing in general. But upon entering high school, those parts of me just left. In the first week I tried my hardest to make friends and talk to new people, but after a while, I stopped, and retracted into a shell. Starting conversations with new people became a struggle for me.

My mother, who is always very interested in if I am making friends, asked me every day about who I had met. And when I did not come back with any new names, she told me I had to meet at least one new person a day. My mother does not mess around. Every day when I got in the car after school, she would ask for names, and most of the time I was able to give her the name of someone I had managed to have a conversation with.

As time passed, I had started to make new friends and acquaintances on the tennis team. My current two best friends Delia and Menna came from the tennis team, and after the season ended, it was just the three of us. I have no classes with Menna and Delia, which meant that I had to branch out in my own classes, which I managed to do. I met many different people that I could do group projects with and casually converse with, which boosted my confidence.

Recently, I was given some very good advice that I will share here. Questions are your best friend. When you are starting a new relationship, whether it is friends or more than friends, the best way to start conversation and make meaningful connections is to ask questions. And do not be afraid to put yourself out there; the worst thing that can come from that is rejection. Rejection may sting at first, but you will get over it and hopefully realize that that person did not deserve your friendship.

I took that advice with me to the freshman retreat and made new friends that I worked with for the day. I now know five more people better than I did before, and I feel like I can say that the girl I used to be is coming back. I can see her when she says hi to people she knows in the hallways and strikes up casual conversations. I can see her when she joins clubs and immediately starts to interact with other people. I see her almost everywhere now and it makes me very happy and proud of her.
