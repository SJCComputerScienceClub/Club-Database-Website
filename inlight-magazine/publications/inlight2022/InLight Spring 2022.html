Mission Statement

InLight is a student-led platform created to spark conversations, breathe nuance into the bias, and uplift voices that would otherwise go unheard. In the spirit of St. John Baptist de la Salle, InLight aims to serve our community by giving everyone a voice, no matter their background. We hope to unite our diverse DMV community through our common home: St. John’s.

A Letter from the Editors

This year’s return back to what people may consider the “new normal,” has proven to be anything but normal. Because these past couple years have been unique circumstances for everyone, many have struggled with how to cope with the impacts of the pandemic. We see the effects even more so now that the world is attempting to move on from COVID-19’s lasting damage, but it has not been an easy recovery. One thing we know to be true about healing in any situation is that it stems from coming together as a community. This is why we decided to focus on the theme of togetherness for this particular publication. We have come back from a long period of separation and isolation from one another, along with that came an even deeper divide in society. This publication has complied the individual experiences of members in our student body, so that reflective voices can be heard as we move forward. We hear about the hardships endured when feeling isolated, but we also see the beauty in coming together as one after being apart for so long. And with that, we believe St. John’s has played a role in keeping our sense of community alive and has helped with this return to the “new normal” to further continue the Lasallian mission as a family.

- Editors-in-Chief: Nagomi Myers ‘22 and Heidy Martinez ‘22

A Letter to FaceTime

Leila Sarkarzadeh ‘23

Dear FaceTime:

Thank you, from the bottom of my heart. Thank you for allowing me to communicate with my friends who live just up the street and for letting me see my grandparents’ dear faces from across the endless sea. I applaud your ability to keep families together through challenging times and friends updated on the new drama that’s insinuating. But I especially commend you for the awfully hard task of bringing us back together, bringing us back as one. This past year was nothing short of isolation mixed with fear, but the fact that you were there through it all makes that year worth everything and more. Thank you for always being there, for those late night conversations and early morning study sessions; for the hard talks and the ridiculously funny ones. I truly cannot find any other words to describe how grateful I am for all that you did and more, but two words: thank you.

Sincerely,

Leila Sarkarzadeh

ever Beating Heart

We are all the children of nature,

She’s the underlying beating heart for us all.

But for far too long we’ve been taking more than she can give.

A mother begging her children for mercy, have you ever heard of it?

For years and years we’ve lived in this Earth’s harmony, The trees and plants give us the air to breathe.

But in return we’ve knocked them down,

Brought the whole world to the ground.

Human Beings tearing it apart.

The youngest kids are holding each other’s hands and bracing for a fight. Together in this twisted world, wanting to make it right.

A crowd has gathered outside the capital,

They all hold signs reminding us of that unseen nurturer.

Nature’s nurturer.

It’s our turn to nurture her.

This problem seems too big to solve.

These mountains seem too big to move,

But there’s not a lot that all our might couldn’t do.

No human thing could trump all of life, There’s no planet B in sight.

We can’t ask for extra time.

So it’s time to go home.

The prodigal children of Mother Nature, all go running back to her. And all together, we can heal her wounds.

We’ll bring her fever down, give her the care we can.

And then we can start again.

She’ll be the ever-beating heart between us,

We’ll live in harmony with the trees, the sky, and the animal kingdom.

We can come together,

And this Earth can live on forever.

-Quinlan Shattuck '25

The strength of a nurse

Kalei Serra '23

The 3 levels of comfort

The Un-Erasable Scar

Isolation—

so harsh, so unnatural, so pervasive,

and yet ironically, so comforting— taints, tests and tethers the subconscious, which so subtly governs the Heart.

The darkness, the cobwebs had acclimated themselves onto my Heart— once unanchored and stout on Its own—

and now lie as a part of Me.

It sought light, it throbbed for Tenderness.

Afar I saw light shimmering at the door.

Soon I was embraced, my Heart enveloped. Touch...Reunion...Kinship.

The flashlight of Hope, of resumed normalcy, glared. Its brightness, though, wavered.

I stood there waiting to greet the next Guest,

with a similar flashlight,

yet my Heart, so accustomed to isolation, screeched, “Temporary! Temporary!”.

It was Temporary. The dreaded, back-stabbing word.

The darkness had been so deeply nestled,

and the cobwebs so tightly wrapped around my Heart that the blurred, dormant sense of self remained. It would take a while

to adjust,

to recover,

to move-forward.

But while this flashlight overwhelmed my Heart,

it was the Vaseline to my Heart’s Scar.

I no longer stood alone, but rather, in the ambience of others; my Heart Bright and Untied.

The Invisible Wire

The Eyes. The raconteurs. Even with

no patting,

talking

or hugging,

there flowed an informality,

an understanding

and commonality between us;

a testament of our humanness.

Our sight, once near-sighted,

extends and sees beyond the human experience; it sees the intangible.

Imagine if we all had to wear plastic gloves,

sealed to our hands.

Imagine what handshakes would feel like.

Imagine how different human contact would be.

What would exhibit our sheer presence to one another, in a particular ounce of time; a moment?

Eye-contact.

The Invisible Wire.

But how would we seek or receive unmuffled affection?

Our arms and hands, our entire bodies, are physical barriers. Protection,

Love

and Security

is what they stand as, for babies,

for adults

and for elders.

But eyes bear the pain once so bravely endured. Eyes provide Comfort. And Recognition.

Eyes see everything,

even the arc of that stranger’s eyebrows.

Eyes are the cheval glass of the human experience. Eyes reflect,

and connect.

Previously blurry, our eyes,

and their encounters,

are what we call, “Togetherness”.

A Familiar, Waiting Hug

The unrestrained, natural embrace of your family, so recognizable to the

grown-

up

human

unfurled into an impulse to desire unthreatened protection as once felt in the hospital room at 8:28AM.

From cradle to care bed,

we have

come

of

age

in appearance, knowledge and experience,

but our inner child still seeks,

still strives.

Our family’s embrace,

at times dismissed, comforts, protects and fuels our pneuma.

Deep-down

the warm enfolds by our mother, our father, or our friend, is still

summoned.

It is, though,

the idea, the certainty,

of being able to be hugged, or kissed or cuddled that is the greatest comfort of all.

At times of

separation,

possibility is like a bulletproof bandaid.

Material objects can, too,

bring comfort to a wounded confidence

and to a teased sense of hope.

A bed,

slept on night after night,

cried on sleepless night after sleepless night,

laughed on sleepover after sleepover,

read on day after day,

seen time and time again,

was a source of comfort merely due to its familiarity and the notion it would be there every morning,

every afternoon and

every night.

It is the idea of togetherness, of laughter, of familiarity

that are the very fibers of comfort.

- Leire Sofia Rossi-Crespo '23
