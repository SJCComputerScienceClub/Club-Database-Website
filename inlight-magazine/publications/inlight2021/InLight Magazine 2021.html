Mission Statement

Inlight is a student-led platform created to spark conversations, breathe nuance into the bias, and uplift voices that would otherwise go unheard. In the spirit of St. John Baptist de la Salle, InLight aims to serve our community by giving everyone a voice, no matter their background. We hope to unite our diverse dmv community through our common home: St. John’s.

Letter from the Editor

This year has proved to be one of challenges and upsets as the St. John’s community has encountered many struggles but still managed to find ways to adapt to these growing changes and difficulties, whether it be from creating lesson plans adaptable to hybrid learning, to walking into a half-full classroom not knowing anyone in the room. For this spring season, I wanted to celebrate everything that we as individuals and a community have been able to accomplish, hence the decision of making the theme growth and awareness as it is our duty as global citizens to become aware of the struggles of others and fight for a positive change in the world, while constantly growing into ourselves as we transition from childhood into adulthood. We have accomplished and grown so much in the face of adversity And deserve to celebrate ourselves. A huge thanks to Ms. Gelso, Our moderator, who has supported InLight from the very beginning and to heidy martinez ‘22 and Nagomi Myers ‘22 who will be taking over as editor-in-chiefs for the 2021-2022 school year. My hope is that these stories and art will open people’s eyes and ears to new experiences and help everyone to become more aware of the world around them and to learn more about their neighbors, just as our Lasallian education calls us to do so.

- Editor-in-Chief: Cristina Racanelli ‘21

Flip of a Coin

Have you ever wished to be something you are not?

Wished to be pretty; wished to be smart.

Wished to be seen,

As being unique—apart.

-

What I wish, is to be simple.

To be normal, To be bland.

But why would you want that?

I do not understand.

-

The truth is,

I am not normal, nor simple,

Nor bland.

I am different; I am a blend.

-

“Where are you from?”

They say.

I am from here, just like you.

“No, where are you really from?” They argue.

-

However—that is not the question to ask.

If takes its true form, it morphs into:

Who are you?

-

I am a coin, I have two faces.

Asian and Caucasian, these are my races.

Not entirely one, not wholly the other.

So what does that make me?

-

Some would say it makes me unique, a novelty.

But is unique,

Really all that it’s cut out to be?

-

Sometimes I wish,

To only be one side of that coin.

Simply Chinese, or simply white.

It would be much easier, right?

-

But I cannot choose to be only one side or the other.

Because I am two sides of the same coin.

I am a mixture of two cultures,

Two races: conjoined.

-

Although it may be difficult,

To embrace both sides,

I have to, I must—

Attempt to try.

-

I am a coin,

Two sides,

Two faces.

-

Each side is vital,

For without one,

The coin is valuable to none.

-Elizabeth Chen ‘24

Accepting Myself

My hands kept fiddling with the tablecloth as I went over the words in my head. The knot in my stomach grew larger and larger with every second. Why was I so nervous? You’re in a much better situation that other young queer people. Even knowing that fact, unease still tensed my muscles up. “I think I’m bi.”, I blurted out. My sister stopped the fork making its way to her mouth. “Wait, what?”. “I’m bisexual. I like guys, gals, and non-binary pals?” Her face changed to a one filled with love. “Hon, that’s great! Oh my god, why didn’t you tell me sooner?”. She went on to ramble about how we could go to Pride together and if I’ve seen any cute girls lately. Relief immediately washed over me. My sister was the first person I came out to and it kind of made us grow closer. The knot in my stomach untangled itself, but it wasn’t gone for good. The dread of having to come out to the rest of the people in my life sat in the back of my head like a boulder waiting for a mud slide. What if they won’t take it as well as she did? I was only in 6th grade at the time so I was expecting the whole “you don’t know who you are yet and you’re too young” speech. But who cares? Maybe I’m not bisexual. I haven’t fully found myself yet. But that’s that magic of growing up and figuring out who you are. Acceptance is a thing that all people want, especially LGBT people. Having a place where they belong and are loved seems like the greatest thing in the world. And it is. But you first have to accept yourself for who you are before other people can.

It wasn’t all rainbows and unicorns though. I remember sitting in a foreign church on a sweltering North Carolina day in seventh grade, counting the seconds till the hour was up. The pastor was currently in the middle of his sermon when he said a phrase that made my ears perk up. “That’s why God doesn’t condone homosexuality.” What? A silent rage started to simmer inside me, mixed with unusual shame. All I wanted to do in that moment was jump up and start arguing with him. I should have known better. It was a small church in North Carolina. On the other hand, I also wanted to curl up in a ball and sob. Does God really hate me? After many months of self-doubt and questioning my faith, I assured myself that God loves everyone because we were “made in his image and likeness”, mirroring the words of my grandmother. No. My pride wasn’t about to be beaten down by a homophobic priest who I barely knew. The world needs to be changed, but I knew that me yelling about prejudice and ignorance to a bunch of people who have been willingly blind won’t do anything except make me look like a naive child. So I calmly stood up and walked out. Discovering this new truth about myself has made me almost more confident. I know who I am at the moment and nothing anyone says or does can change that. Like a radio being turned down, the doubts in my mind subside. There’s so much ignorance and pure hate in the world right now that it’s difficult for people like me to feel comfortable just existing. Why should we feel the need to hide or make other people comfortable? Questions like these are the ones that need to be asked. My experiences in the LGBT+ community have been far tamer than others. But that shouldn’t be the case. Queer people shouldn’t have to fear being kicked out of their houses

by their own family for being gay, nor should they feel the need to argue their existence to person who can’t mind their business. It’s enraging but at the same time jus exhausting. We shouldn’t have to fight, but we do. Acceptance is a thing that all people want, especially LGBT people. Having a place where they belong and are loved seems like the greatest thing in the world. And it is. But you first have to accept yourself for who you are before other people can.

-Anonymous

Objectification on My Own School Bus

The school bus in eighth grade honestly seemed like the seventh ring of hell whenever I stepped on. I was awkward and uncomfortable with my body, but being in middle school the attention from the boys on my bus felt like the only way I could gain any more confidence in myself. In the morning things were usually pretty calm, but once everyone had energy in the afternoon things would escalate. The boys would yell racist slurs, grab the girls upper thighs without asking, as a joke, and play the amusing game of trying to find where our nipples were under our shirts. To be completely honest, I did very little to end any of this. If anything, I felt somewhat proud of myself that I was pretty enough to be the subject of their attention (which is horrible). If anything, reflecting on this experience makes me more conscious of a larger problem in society. Young girls have been so influenced by the media that they seek validation from males so much to the point of accepting sexual harassment. I don’t mean that this was any of our faults, it was one hundred percent the actions of the boys, but it still is significant that I failed to act against the harassment. This experience also shows how our society harms both boys and girls. Thirteen year old boys feel the need to boost their confidence by sexually assaulting thirteen year old girls. When I think about the bus situation, I can’t help but wonder if I would act the same towards the girls under peer pressure if I was one of the boys. My eighth grade school bus experience was trivial when compared to the horrific acts of violence that women endure everyday, but it is still an example of the many small acts of harassment that almost any women will experience in their lives, and highlights how important it is for young boys to be educated on sexual harassment.

-Celia Chorzempa ‘21

The Proximity of Poverty

As my family and I drove across the highway, I could see miles and miles of farmland out of my window. The only cars that passed us by were pick-up trucks, many of which were not new. The few billboards we passed were religious advertisements, and I hadn’t seen a gas station or a grocery store for miles.

Out of nowhere, a brand new billboard loomed ahead of us with a shiny new advertisement for Rolex. Suddenly we were being passed by Volvos, BMWs, and Mercedes cars as shopping centers, cafes, and grocery stores popped up left and right. The fields and farm animals were replaced by affluence in only a few miles as we entered the city of Charlottesville.

I was shocked by the stark contrast between poverty and wealth which could be seen by driving just a few miles. However, this experience is not isolated to the city of Charlottesville and its outskirts. It is common for areas of economic prosperity to exist next to neighborhoods that are far less wealthy. This disparity can be seen through cities around the country, including areas in D.C. where the wealthy and impoverished exist only a few minutes apart.

It is not necessary to travel to a third world country to witness poverty. The reality of the wealth gap in the United States is clearly displayed through areas such as Charlottesville where the difference in wealth is as if a line has been drawn between the rich and poor. We cannot turn a blind eye to those who are suffering in neighborhoods so close to where we live. In order to be an ally, it is important to understand the experiences of those around you, and acknowledge that poverty is closer than you think.

-Bailey Middleton ‘21

My Beautiful Body

A few months ago I was looking through old photos and I found a picture of myself from when I was in elementary school, when I went to the beach with my family. Seeing this picture really struck me because I vividly remember feeling self conscious in my bathing suit and uncomfortable with my body; but when I looked at the picture, all I could see was what a cute kid I was. My thighs were perfect even though I would always compare their size to other girls. The gap between my teeth was adorable even though I was embarrassed about it. The size of my stomach was not the size of a watermelon even though I thought it was. In that moment I genuinely could not understand my younger self’s bodily insecurities. I was healthy and yet around that age I began to be unable to recognize how strong and powerful my body was and instead focused on all the things I disliked about it.

Despite all of the body positivity that is becoming more and more prevalent in society, girls (boys as well, but I can only speak from my own experience and the people I have talked to) all over the country struggle with coming to terms with their bodies. I honestly cannot name a single girl that I know who has not struggled with body image, thought she was fat, thought she was too buff, or even had an eating disorder. This drive for the perfect body is a disease that eats us up, consuming thoughts and mentalities. It is what forces girls to start working out before summer begins so that they can achieve the idealized “beach body”. It is the answer to what the boys in my class asked me as to why their dates rarely eat before homecoming or prom despite there being plenty of catered food for everyone to eat. It is why some girls miss their periods from exercising without receiving the proper caloric intake. It is why girls start dieting as young as middle school. It is why girls feel guilty for getting seconds when eating out or for getting a burger when everyone else is eating salads.

After lamenting over the loss of the acceptance of my body as a youth, I decided that it was time for me to start looking at my current body like I will when I see a picture of myself ten years from now, seeing all the beauty and strength that I could not always recognize. I decided that in the future when I look back at pictures of my high school self, I will not regret spending all the time that I did not truly appreciate and thanking my body for all that it has, can, and will achieve; but instead be grateful for all the time I spent truly loving myself.

-Cristina Racanelli ‘21
